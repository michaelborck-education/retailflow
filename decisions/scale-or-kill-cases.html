Scale or Kill: Strategic Decision Cases

Evaluate three AI/automation projects and make strategic decisions about resource allocation

Case 1: Predictive Inventory System
PILOT PHASE
Project Overview

An AI system that predicts inventory needs across 50 retail locations based on weather, local events, historical patterns, and social media sentiment. Launched 9 months ago in 15 stores.

Current Performance Metrics
Forecast Accuracy (vs. Current Method): +23% better predictions
Inventory Carrying Costs: -$340K annually in pilot stores
Out-of-Stock Incidents: -18% (vs. -12% target)
Implementation & Maintenance Cost: $85K annually
Store Manager Adoption Rate: 72% (some override predictions)
Data Quality Issues: 8% of predictions affected by bad data feeds
Scale Arguments
  • Strong ROI: +$255K net savings in 15 stores annually
  • Easy to expand to all 50 locations without major rework
  • Competitive advantage if competitors lack this
  • Staff training mostly complete
  • Better customer satisfaction (less stock-outs)
Kill Arguments
  • Data dependency creates ongoing maintenance costs
  • 28% of store managers not fully trusting system
  • Requires constant model retraining for seasonal changes
  • Scaling to 50 stores would increase maintenance by 200%
  • Supply chain disruptions make model predictions unreliable

Key Decision Factors

  • Financial Impact: $255K annual savings vs. $85K maintenance cost = excellent ROI
  • Operational Complexity: Medium - requires data governance and model monitoring
  • Strategic Fit: High - aligns with operational efficiency goals
  • Risk Level: Low-Medium - existing pilot proven the concept
Group Discussion Prompts
  • Would you scale this? Why or why not?
  • What's your confidence level in the 23% accuracy improvement holding at scale?
  • How would you address the 28% of store managers not trusting the system?
  • What's your risk tolerance for ongoing maintenance costs?
Your Decision:
Facilitator Note

This is the strongest case for scaling. The math works, risk is low, and it addresses core business needs. Most groups should vote to scale. Use this to discuss risk tolerance and financial decision-making frameworks. If groups kill this, probe why and discuss whether they're being too conservative.

Case 2: AI Customer Service Chatbot
EXPERIMENTAL
Project Overview

A large language model-based chatbot deployed to handle tier-1 customer service inquiries (returns, shipping, product info). Launched 4 months ago on the website and mobile app.

Current Performance Metrics
Customer Satisfaction (chatbot vs. human agent): Chatbot 62% vs. Human 89%
First Contact Resolution Rate: 43% (target was 60%)
Escalation Rate to Human Agents: 37% of chats (vs. 15% target)
Monthly Operating Cost: $32K (API calls + infrastructure)
Human Agent Labor Savings: $12K monthly (from handling 25% fewer tier-1 calls)
Hallucination/False Info Rate: 3-4% of responses contain inaccurate information
Customer Complaints About Chatbot: Increased 280% vs. pre-launch baseline
Scale Arguments
  • Scales handling 24/7 (humans can't)
  • Customer base expects AI support
  • Small labor cost savings compound over time
  • Technology improves quickly (LLMs getting better)
  • Could integrate with inventory/order systems
Kill Arguments
  • Costing $20K/month more than it saves
  • 27-point satisfaction gap vs. human agents
  • Hallucination risk creates liability exposure
  • 37% escalation rate defeats efficiency purpose
  • Brand damage from poor customer experiences
  • Market sentiment turning against chatbots

Key Decision Factors

  • Financial Impact: -$20K monthly net loss (unacceptable)
  • Operational Complexity: High - requires constant monitoring for errors/hallucinations
  • Strategic Fit: Medium - customer expectations + brand risk
  • Risk Level: High - poor customer satisfaction + liability concerns
Group Discussion Prompts
  • The math clearly says kill this. Do you agree? Would you take any other action?
  • How do you weigh the 27-point satisfaction gap vs. 24/7 availability?
  • Is the hallucination rate (3-4%) acceptable in customer service? Why/why not?
  • If you were going to kill it, how would you communicate to customers?
Your Decision:
Facilitator Note

This should be an easier kill decision than Case 1. The metrics are clearly negative - financial loss, poor satisfaction, and high escalation. However, some groups may want to pivot or improve. Discuss: at what point does a failing AI project get the axe? When do sunk costs matter vs. future potential? This raises questions about organizational tolerance for AI experiments.

Case 3: AI Personalization Engine
BETA
Project Overview

A machine learning system that personalizes product recommendations, homepage content, and email offers based on browsing behavior, purchase history, and similar customer segments. Launched 6 months ago; deployed to 40% of traffic.

Current Performance Metrics
Conversion Rate (personalized vs. control): +8.2% improvement (statistically significant)
Average Order Value: +$4.30 per order (+6% vs. control)
Development & Infrastructure Cost: $120K one-time, $25K/month ongoing
Payback Period: ~18 months based on current revenue lift
Data Privacy Compliance Issues: Under GDPR/CCPA audit; potential exposure
Model Drift (accuracy degradation): 2% monthly accuracy decline (being investigated)
Customer Concerns About Privacy: 12% of surveyed customers uncomfortable with targeting
Scale Arguments
  • +8.2% conversion lift is significant
  • Revenue impact easily justifies $25K/month cost
  • Payback period acceptable for enterprise AI
  • Competitive necessity - others doing this too
  • Can improve model accuracy with more data
Kill Arguments
  • Regulatory exposure still being assessed
  • 12% customer discomfort signals brand risk
  • Model drift trending negatively
  • High customer acquisition cost if privacy concerns grow
  • Alternative: A/B test improvements before scale

Key Decision Factors

  • Financial Impact: Positive ROI (revenue lift >> maintenance costs) but 18-month payback
  • Operational Complexity: Medium-High - model maintenance + compliance monitoring required
  • Strategic Fit: High - core to personalization roadmap
  • Risk Level: Medium - regulatory uncertainty + customer sentiment + technical drift
Group Discussion Prompts
  • Would you scale despite regulatory uncertainty? When should you wait for clarity?
  • The 12% customer discomfort - is that a dealbreaker or acceptable?
  • How concerned are you about the 2% monthly accuracy decline? What would you do?
  • Is an 18-month payback period reasonable for this company's investment horizon?
  • Could you scale to 100% while improving compliance simultaneously?
Your Decision:
Facilitator Note

This is the most nuanced case - financially it looks good, but regulatory and customer sentiment risks are real. Most mature organizations would choose HOLD (resolve compliance, then scale). This case teaches risk-balanced decision-making. Discuss: Is it better to win fast and manage risk, or move slowly? What's your company's risk tolerance?

Decision-Making Framework Summary

When to SCALE
  • Strong financial ROI (revenue/savings >> costs)
  • Low or manageable technical risk
  • Clear path to expansion without major rework
  • Customer/team adoption is solid
  • Regulatory/compliance issues resolved
When to KILL
  • Financial ROI negative or unacceptable payback period
  • High customer satisfaction gaps or complaints
  • Unresolved safety/compliance issues
  • Technical problems worsening (not improving)
  • Better alternative solutions available
When to HOLD/PIVOT
  • Mixed signals requiring more data or investigation
  • Can make targeted improvements while continuing pilot
  • Compliance/regulatory issues need resolution first
  • Market conditions changing (wait and see)
  • Alternative path that could work better (pivot, don't kill)
